# Project Retrospective

## What Went Well

One thing that went well during this project was staying organized by breaking the work into smaller phases. Completing the planning documents first gave me a clear direction before I started building the website. I also tested my work often, which helped me find and fix problems early. Getting feedback before the final submission helped me improve the overall quality of the website.

---

## What Went Wrong

The biggest challenge I faced was spending more time than expected trying to get the website to look exactly the way I wanted. I also ran into a few issues with GitHub Pages and image formatting that took some trial and error to fix. Even though these problems were frustrating at times, they helped me become more comfortable with troubleshooting and using GitHub.

---

## What I Would Do Differently

If I completed this project again, I would start by clearly identifying my target audience before creating any content. Since this website is designed for people who have never been to a Texas Tech football game in Lubbock, I would ask members of that audience to review the website after each phase of the project. I would ask questions like, "Does this guide help you feel prepared for game day?" and "Is there any information you wish was included?" Their feedback would help me understand whether the website is actually useful or if it feels too basic.

I would also include more project reviews after each phase, regardless of who is providing the feedback. The more people who review the project, the easier it is to identify gaps, catch things I may have overlooked, and find opportunities to make the project better. I learned that regular feedback throughout a project is much more valuable than waiting until the end because it gives you the chance to improve as you go.
